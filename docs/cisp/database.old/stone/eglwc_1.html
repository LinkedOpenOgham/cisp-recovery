Names

  • Avitoria (Language: celtic; Gender: female)
    Jackson/1953, 185: `The name is not known in later British or Irish, though it does occur in the masculne form, genitive, in [Macalister/1945] no. 396, AVITORI. It is uncertain to which language it belongs and even what the name really is'. Jackson/1953, 186 suggests that AVITORIA might be a 'feminine derivative in *-o-riga of a British *Auitorix, and that it so `the Latin would stand for [auidoriga] or [auidorija], with the lenitedg or j lost or abasorbed very early, or at least ignored after the i'.

    For earlier discussion of the origin of AVITORIA see Rhys/1889, 228--229.

  • Cunigni (Language: Brittonic; Gender: male)
    Jackson/1953, 174: `*Cunignos...is British rather than Irish, becoming regularly Cynin in Welsh'.

    Jackson/1953, 461, 463, argues that this name indicates that -g- had not yet been vocalised before -n-. Jackson/1953, 670, cites CVNIGNI as evidence for Cun-. Also see Thomas/1994, 75.

Language:Goidelic (oghms)
Ling. Notes:This inscription has the only appearance of the Primitive Irish word INIGENA for 'daughter'. Jackson/1953, 185, sees INIGENA, `daughter' as nominative and states that AVITTORIGES `is agreed to be genitive'.
Paleo. Notes:Macalister/1945, 346--347, 'Apparently the inverted construction is intended to bring the words of the Ogham translation adjacent to the corresponding Roman words. The fifth letter was first blocked out as an I, but only the first four notches were deepened to their full depth. Evidently the engraver hesitated to deface the erroneous score completely, lest he should make difficulties for himself by breaking the smooth surfaceof the stone. The A of AVITTORIGES is a long score not a notch'.
Legibility:good
The only point of contention has been the number of vowel strokes after the G in INIGENA. Rhys/1889, 224 argued there were five strokes, but Macalister [Macalister/1921, 22; Macalister/1945, 346] has argued that the fifth stroke was only lightly carved after the lapidary recognised that only four were needed. This was accepted by Nash-Williams/1950, 109, and fig. 112.
