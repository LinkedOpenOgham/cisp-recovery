SISHM/1

(The Llansaint stone)
Corpus Refs:Macalister/1945:376
Nash-Williams/1950:174
Site:SISHM
Discovery:in/on structure, 1846 Francis, G.G.
History:Still in position where it was first seen. According to Rhys/1907, 72--73, the stone has been built into the church since before 1862.

Westwood/1879, 85, speaks of a sketch made of the stone at this spot in 1846 by the brother of Col. G.G. Francis, the man who had alerted Westwood to the stone in 1875.

Macalister/1945, 358: `Built into the outer face of the S. wall of the nave of the parish church, low down and close to the ground, almost under the second window from the W. end'.

Form:plain
Condition:incomplete , some
The left edge of the stone is quite badly damaged, so that the bottom of the second line of text is lost, while flaking continues around the top and down the right side of the stone as well.

Macalister/1945, 359, argues that the flaking was intentional and was designed to rid the stone of an Ogham inscription.

Names

  • Vennisetli (Language: Brittonic; Gender: male)
    Jackson/1953, 325, 521, argues that the SETLI part of the name represents a stage in *saitlo>hoedl, with -ai- > -e- and s- > h- in initial position.

    Jackson/1953, 512, sees -nn- for earlier -nd-. Thomas/1994, 95, sees Vennisetli as a British name.

  • Ercagnas (Language: Goidelic; Gender: male)
    White/1971-72, 46--51, citing personal communications from Jackson and Melville Richards, argues that Ercagni is from Latin *Ercangus or British *Ercagnos giving Erchan in Welsh. White quotes Jackson as stating `the name of the dead man, Ercagnos, is certainly an Irish one, though this naturally does not mean that this particular man was Irish'. He also cites Richards as arguing that since *Erc and -agn- appear in both British and Irish, a choice of origin is not possible.

    Edwards/1986, 23, argues the name to be Irish.

    The above references are to the ARFYN/1 stone in Anglesey, but the name is identical.

    Thomas/1994, 95, sees Ercagn(as) as Irish.
