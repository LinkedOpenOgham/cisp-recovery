Names

  • DEVORIGI (Language: Brittonic; Gender: male)
    Sims-Williams in White/Smith/1999, 147--148: `The name on the stone is not otherwise attested in Britain. DERVORI, on a now lost stone from Llannor parish, Llyn (Nash-Williams, no. 94 = Macalister no. 388), happens to be transcribed immediately after DEVORIGI on fol. 88r of Stowe 1023, but is linguistically distinct. .. The first element of DEVORIGI (genitive of *Devorix) is Celtic *deiwos `god' (cf. Jackson/1953, 375), as in the Old Welsh name Duinerth `divine strength' (Evans/Rhys/1893, 396), so that the whole name might be translated `divine king'. It is a personal name and should certainly not be regarded as a title, nor as an indication of paganism .. In later Welsh the name would be *Dwywyr or *Dwywri, depending on whether the nominative or an oblique case survived .. The long close E of the first syllable may already have become /ui/ in speech in the sixth century, but there is no evidence that the dipthong was shown in writing until the eighth century (Sims-Williams/1991: 49 and 59), so the E spelling proves little.

    The preservation of the composition vowel -O- with the correct quality is typical of inscriptions which Jackson dated to the fifth and sixth centuries but the possibility of such a conservative spelling in the seventh century cannot be ruled out entirely. The form -RIGI instead on the original British genitive *rigos is paralleled in inscriptions which Jackson dated to the fifth and sixth centuries and it seems likely that our inscription is contemporary with these. Admittedly, final -g was sometimes written in the Old Welsh period (Jackson/1953: 456 cites dou rig in the Historia Brittonum), yet the possibility that DEVORIGI is an artificial Latinized genitive in -i of a form such as Archaic Old Welsh *Deurig, Old Welsh *Duiuri(g), complete with correctly restored composition vowel, seems remote'.
